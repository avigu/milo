## 1. Overview

Cameco Corporation is the world's largest publicly traded uranium company and the second-largest uranium producer globally by volume. Headquartered in Saskatoon, Saskatchewan, the company operates some of the highest-grade uranium mines on earth — McArthur River and Cigar Lake in Saskatchewan's Athabasca Basin — alongside a 49% stake in Westinghouse Electric Company, a global nuclear fuel fabrication and reactor services business acquired jointly with Brookfield Asset Management in late 2023.

The nuclear renaissance is not a speculative narrative; it is an industry reality confirmed by decade-long supply deficits, government policy reversals on nuclear energy across Europe and North America, and the signing of long-term power purchase agreements by major technology companies seeking 24/7 carbon-free electricity to power AI data centers. Cameco sits at the center of this structural shift, and its stock — up 137% over the past 52 weeks — reflects significant market recognition of that position.

The analytical challenge for Cameco is not whether the thesis is correct. It is. The challenge is price. At $116.75 per share, Cameco trades at 117.9x trailing P/E, 85.5x forward P/E, and 81.4x EV/EBITDA. These are not energy company multiples; they are growth software company multiples applied to a mining business. The thesis requires not only being right about the nuclear renaissance — it requires being right at a price that already prices in a significant portion of that renaissance.

The recommendation is HOLD for current shareholders and WAIT for new investors — specifically waiting for a pullback toward the $90-100 range before adding exposure, at which point the risk/reward becomes substantially more attractive.

## 2. Strategic Position

Cameco has transformed itself over the past five years from a troubled uranium miner that mothballed its flagship McArthur River mine in 2018 (amid multi-year uranium price depression) into the dominant pure-play nuclear energy investment vehicle available to global equity investors.

**Mine Portfolio:** McArthur River/Key Lake is the world's largest high-grade uranium mine, with ore grades averaging more than 6% U3O8 — roughly 100 times the global average for uranium deposits. This is not merely a technical distinction; it translates directly into dramatically lower production costs per pound. Cigar Lake, the second-highest-grade uranium mine in the world, operates at comparable quality. These two assets together represent a geological endowment that cannot be replicated by competitors.

**Westinghouse Electric (49% stake):** The Westinghouse acquisition transformed Cameco from a commodity producer into a nuclear value-chain company. Westinghouse provides AP1000 reactor technology (the dominant design for new reactor construction in the U.S., Poland, Ukraine, and elsewhere), nuclear fuel fabrication for hundreds of existing reactors, and maintenance and services to the global reactor fleet. This asset generates more stable, higher-margin revenues than mining and provides significant earnings predictability. Westinghouse's revenue base is largely contracted under long-term service agreements with nuclear utilities.

**Long-Term Contracting Strategy:** Cameco has been deliberately rebuilding its long-term sales book at dramatically higher price levels than the legacy contracts signed in the 2010s. New contracts are being signed at $70-80 per pound U3O8, compared to legacy book prices of $30-50 per pound. As low-priced legacy contracts roll off over 2026-2029 and are replaced by new contracts at current market prices, Cameco's revenue per pound will increase substantially without any increase in production volume. This earnings growth pipeline is built-in and does not require additional operational execution.

**Inkai Joint Venture:** Cameco holds a 40% interest in the Inkai uranium mine in Kazakhstan, operated in partnership with Kazatomprom. Kazakhstan is the world's largest uranium producer, and Inkai adds low-cost production to Cameco's portfolio. However, the Kazakhstan supply chain has faced disruptions related to logistics (routing challenges post-Russia-Ukraine conflict) and Kazatomprom's own permitting and ramp-up difficulties — creating supply uncertainty that has contributed to elevated spot prices.

## 3. AI Exposure

The connection between artificial intelligence and uranium demand is real, direct, and increasingly acknowledged by the utility sector.

AI model training requires continuous, uninterruptible power at industrial scale. A hyperscale data center campus drawing 300-500 megawatts cannot tolerate the intermittency of solar or wind generation without massive and expensive battery storage buffers. Nuclear power, by contrast, generates electricity at approximately 90% capacity factor on a continuous basis — 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, regardless of weather. For a data center operator making multi-decade capital commitments, nuclear power is the optimal baseload electricity source.

The most concrete validation of this thesis came in late 2023 when Microsoft signed a 20-year power purchase agreement (PPA) to restart Three Mile Island's Unit 1 reactor in Pennsylvania — a plant that had been shuttered in 2019. This is not a pilot program or a demonstration project; it is a 20-year, utility-scale commitment by the world's most sophisticated technology company to purchase carbon-free nuclear power for its data center infrastructure. Amazon, Google, and Meta have made similar commitments to nuclear power development through various PPAs, advanced nuclear technology investments, and corporate energy strategies.

Each reactor restart or new nuclear build requires uranium fuel. The existing U.S. fleet of 93 operating reactors consumes approximately 50 million pounds of U3O8 per year. If the United States adds 10-15% reactor capacity over the next decade through restarts and new builds — a conservative estimate given current policy and utility commitments — incremental annual uranium demand increases by 5-7 million pounds. Against a global production supply that has struggled to reach 150 million pounds per year, this demand increment is significant.

Cameco, as the largest Western uranium producer and the owner of the world's highest-grade deposits, captures this demand growth through both higher sales volumes and higher contract prices.

## 4. Competitive Moat

Cameco's competitive moat derives from the irreplaceable quality of its assets and the structural barriers to uranium mining.

**Geological Moat:** McArthur River and Cigar Lake are the highest-grade uranium mines in the world. High-grade ore means lower operating costs, lower environmental impact per pound produced, and longer mine life per unit of capital invested. These assets took decades to discover, permit, and develop. No competing producer can replicate this geological endowment through capital expenditure or technology — the grade is a function of geology, not human decision-making.

**Permitting and Regulatory Moat:** Uranium mining requires extensive federal and provincial regulatory approval in Canada and equivalent approvals in other jurisdictions. The Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC) process for new uranium mine permitting is measured in years to decades, not months. This creates an enormous barrier to entry for any new competitor seeking to bring high-volume, high-grade Canadian uranium production to market.

**Technical Expertise Moat:** Mining ore that grades above 50% U3O8 — as at McArthur River — requires highly specialized remote mining techniques, radiation management protocols, and extraction technology. Cameco and its Cigar Lake partner Orano are the only companies in the world with demonstrated expertise in this type of operation.

**Westinghouse Brand Moat:** Westinghouse's AP1000 reactor design is the only Generation III+ reactor with U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission design certification. Its installed base of fuel fabrication contracts with hundreds of global utilities creates switching-cost-based recurring revenue. Nuclear utilities do not change fuel suppliers casually — the qualification process is technically demanding and expensive.

**Long-Term Contract Lock-in:** The multi-year sales contracts being signed at $70-80/lb create a revenue stream that is neither immediately re-priceable downward nor subject to short-term commodity price volatility. This forward contracted book provides earnings visibility and predictability that pure spot-price-exposed producers cannot match.

## 5. Financial Analysis

Cameco's financial profile in May 2026 reflects a company in transition — moving from legacy low-price contracts toward a higher-priced book — which creates the earnings growth trajectory that justifies investor interest despite elevated multiples.

**Income Statement:**
- Revenue TTM: $3.5 billion (+11% YoY) — growth reflects both higher uranium prices and Westinghouse consolidation (Westinghouse was acquired in late 2023; TTM comparisons are therefore still being affected by partial-year comparisons)
- Q1 2026 revenue growth: +7.1% — modest near-term growth as legacy contracts still represent a significant portion of sales
- Gross Margin: 27.9% — reflects the blended effect of lower-priced legacy mining contracts and higher-margin Westinghouse services revenue
- Operating Margin: 17.8% — solid for a mining company but modest for a business priced at 80x+ EBITDA
- Net Margin: 16.9% — slightly below operating margin due to interest expense on acquisition debt
- EBITDA: $894 million — the key reference point for valuation

**Earnings Performance:**
- EPS TTM: $1.35 (implying 117.9x P/E — very expensive)
- EPS Growth YoY: +243.6% — reflecting the recovery from depressed 2024 levels
- Q1 2026: $0.46 vs. $0.37 consensus (+24% beat) — strong
- Q4 2025: $0.50 vs. $0.44 consensus (+14% beat) — solid
- Q3 2025: $0.07 vs. $0.31 consensus (substantial miss) — this quarter requires careful interpretation
- The Q3 miss was driven by delivery timing. Cameco books revenue when uranium is physically delivered to customers, not when contracts are signed. Quarterly delivery scheduling is lumpy — a delayed shipment pushes revenue into the following quarter. This is an accounting timing issue, not a business quality issue.

**Forward Earnings Path:**
The earnings growth case for Cameco is not about FY2026 — it is about 2027-2029 as legacy low-priced contracts roll off. Each year, several million pounds of below-market contracted uranium expires and is replaced by new contracts at $70-80/lb. The math is straightforward: if Cameco sells 30 million pounds annually and the average realized price increases from $50/lb to $75/lb over five years, annual revenue from uranium alone increases by $750 million. This is why forward P/E compresses meaningfully in out-years even as trailing P/E appears extreme.

**Balance Sheet:**
- Debt/Equity: 0.14 — very low; the Westinghouse acquisition was financed in part with equity and Brookfield partnership structures
- Cameco's balance sheet conservatism is a genuine strength in a capital-intensive mining business

## 6. Bottlenecks

**Delivery Lumpiness:** As demonstrated by the Q3 2025 miss, Cameco's revenue recognition tied to physical uranium deliveries creates inherent quarter-to-quarter volatility. This makes short-term earnings per share an unreliable measure of business progress and creates volatility around earnings dates.

**McArthur River Ramp:** The McArthur River mine was restarted in 2022 after being on care-and-maintenance since 2018. Ramping a high-grade underground uranium mine to full capacity is technically complex. As of 2026, the mine is operating but below its licensed maximum capacity of approximately 18 million pounds per year. Full ramp-up requires continued investment in equipment, workforce training, and mine development — a 12-24 month process.

**Kazakhstan Supply Chain:** The Inkai JV's production has been periodically disrupted by logistical challenges related to the Russia-Ukraine conflict's impact on traditional shipping routes (the Caspian Pipeline Consortium route was affected). Alternative routing through China is more expensive and slower. These disruptions have contributed to uranium spot price strength but also create operational unpredictability in Cameco's production figures.

**Westinghouse Integration:** The Westinghouse acquisition closed in November 2023, and full operational and financial integration is ongoing. Complexity arises from Westinghouse's global operations across multiple jurisdictions, its significant workforce (approximately 9,000 employees), and the technical demands of managing a nuclear fuel fabrication business alongside a mining operation.

**Financing Costs:** The Westinghouse acquisition required meaningful debt financing and the issuance of additional equity. While the balance sheet is healthy at 0.14x Debt/Equity, interest costs are a drag on net income that did not exist pre-acquisition.

## 7. Industry Trends

The uranium and nuclear power industry is experiencing a structural renaissance driven by three forces: energy security concerns, climate policy, and AI power demand.

**Supply Deficit:** Global uranium mine supply has chronically undershot consumption for most of the past decade. The post-Fukushima shutdown of Japanese reactors, combined with utility destocking, masked this deficit through 2020. As inventories normalized and Japanese reactors restarted, the supply deficit became apparent — driving spot prices from the $30-40/lb range in 2020 to approximately $100-110/lb today. This deficit will take 5-10 years of new mine development to resolve, supporting structurally elevated prices.

**Russia Sanctions:** In 2024, the United States enacted legislation banning the import of Russian-enriched uranium. Russia supplies approximately 35% of global uranium enrichment capacity. The ban forces U.S. utilities to find alternative enrichment sources — at higher cost and with less certainty of supply. This creates additional demand for Western-sourced uranium and enrichment services, directly benefiting Cameco.

**New Reactor Construction:** China is constructing approximately 20 new reactors, with dozens more in various stages of planning and approval. India, South Korea, the UAE, Poland, and the UK all have active nuclear development programs. Each new reactor adds approximately 400,000 pounds per year of uranium demand through its operational life. The cumulative demand from reactors under construction globally represents approximately 20-25 million additional pounds of annual uranium demand by the early 2030s.

**Small Modular Reactors (SMRs):** Companies including NuScale, TerraPower, and Rolls-Royce are developing small modular reactor designs that could eventually be deployed more rapidly and at lower cost than traditional large reactors. SMRs represent a medium-term demand expansion potential for uranium, though commercial deployment at scale is unlikely before 2030-2032.

## 8. Competitive Landscape

Cameco competes in uranium production primarily against Kazatomprom (Kazakhstan, state-owned), Orano (France, state-owned), Uranium One (Russia, state-owned), and a range of smaller junior miners (NexGen Energy, Denison Mines, Boss Energy in Australia, Paladin Energy).

The competitive landscape is dominated by state-owned entities, which gives Cameco a unique position as the largest Western, publicly traded uranium producer. This matters for two reasons: (1) Western utilities increasingly prefer Western-sourced uranium for energy security and ESG reasons; and (2) government sanctions targeting Russian and, increasingly, Kazakh supply chains create structural demand for Cameco's Canadian production.

In nuclear services (Westinghouse), the competitive landscape includes Framatome (France, part of EDF), GE-Hitachi, and BWXT Nuclear. Westinghouse's AP1000 technology leadership and installed base provide durable competitive advantages in this segment.

Junior miners such as NexGen Energy (Rook I project) represent future supply competition that could erode Cameco's pricing power in the 2030s, but these projects are years from production and face the same permitting timelines that protected Cameco's incumbency.

## 9. Regulatory Risk

**Nuclear Liability:** Uranium mining and nuclear fuel services are among the most heavily regulated industries in existence. Changes to nuclear liability frameworks, radiation standards, or mining regulations can increase costs materially. Cameco's Canadian operations are regulated by the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission, which maintains world-class standards but is generally supportive of operating mines at established sites.

**Export Controls:** Uranium is a controlled material under both Canadian and U.S. law. Changes to export licensing requirements or escalating trade tensions between uranium-producing and uranium-consuming nations could disrupt supply chains and increase compliance costs.

**Kazakh Political Risk:** The Inkai JV in Kazakhstan is subject to Kazakh investment law, political developments in Astana, and the government's evolving posture toward foreign resource ownership. Kazakhstan's government has periodically renegotiated terms with foreign resource partners. A deterioration in investment conditions could affect Inkai production economics.

**Nuclear Public Policy Risk:** Nuclear power's political support has improved dramatically since 2020, but public opinion remains sensitive to nuclear accidents. A major nuclear incident anywhere in the world — even unrelated to any Cameco facility — could trigger public opposition that slows reactor restarts or new builds, reducing uranium demand growth.

**Westinghouse Nuclear Regulation:** Westinghouse's fuel fabrication and services operations are subject to Nuclear Regulatory Commission oversight in the U.S. and equivalent bodies internationally. Any facility incident or compliance issue could result in operating license suspension — a low-probability but high-impact risk.

## 10. Valuation

This is where the investment case for Cameco becomes complicated. The business is excellent. The thesis is correct. The valuation is demanding.

**Current Multiples:**
- P/E TTM: 117.9x — pricing in extraordinary earnings growth
- Forward P/E: 85.5x — still very expensive even on next-year estimates
- EV/EBITDA: 81.4x — extraordinarily high for a mining/industrial company
- Revenue multiple: approximately 14.5x sales — software-level pricing for a commodity producer

**How Can This Be Justified?**
The bull case for accepting these multiples rests on the earnings growth trajectory embedded in contract repricing. If Cameco's uranium segment average realized price increases from approximately $50/lb (current blended legacy+market) to $75/lb over four years, and production volume holds at 25-30 million pounds annually, uranium segment revenue alone grows by $600-750 million annually. Westinghouse adds stable $1.5-2B in revenue. Combined, forward EBITDA on a 2028-2029 basis could be $2-3B rather than the current $894M — compressing the EV/EBITDA to approximately 17-27x on a forward basis. That is expensive but not insane for a dominant business in a structurally growing market.

**The Problem:** Those 2028-2029 EBITDA numbers require: (1) uranium prices staying near $100/lb; (2) McArthur River ramp-up executing on schedule; (3) new high-priced contracts being signed as legacy ones expire; and (4) Westinghouse continuing to grow. These are all reasonable expectations — but they are fully priced in at $116.75.

**Price Target and Entry Point:**
The analyst consensus target of $138.60 represents 19% upside from current levels — meaningful, but modest for a stock with this level of execution and price risk.

A significantly better entry point would be $90-100 per share, at which the stock would trade at approximately 55-65x forward P/E — still expensive by absolute standards but representing a more reasonable premium given the structural earnings growth ahead. A 10-15% market correction or a single quarterly earnings disappointment (delivery timing, production miss) could create that entry point.

## 11. Risk Analysis

**Primary Risk — Uranium Price Reversal:** The entire Cameco thesis depends on uranium prices staying elevated. If uranium prices fall from $100/lb toward $60-70/lb — driven by faster-than-expected Kazakh supply recovery, Chinese demand disappointment, or reactor restart delays — the multiple compression would be severe. The stock ran from $49 to $135 on the uranium bull market; a meaningful portion of that gain could retrace on price weakness.

**Valuation Risk:** At 81x EV/EBITDA, any disappointment in earnings trajectory is punished harshly. The Q3 2025 miss (even though timing-related) caused significant stock volatility. A miss that reflects genuine business deterioration rather than timing would be punished much more severely.

**Kazatomprom Re-acceleration:** If Kazatomprom successfully ramps its production to historical capacity, global uranium supply increases materially, putting downward pressure on spot prices. Kazatomprom has guided for production recovery but has repeatedly missed targets — each miss has been bullish for prices and Cameco's stock.

**Geopolitical Concentration Risk:** Cameco's best mines are in Canada (stable) but its Inkai JV is in Kazakhstan (less stable). Any deterioration in Kazakhstan's political environment, foreign investment framework, or relationship with Western nations could impair Inkai production.

**Westinghouse Integration Risk:** Westinghouse is a complex global business. Integration challenges, unexpected liabilities (nuclear businesses carry significant historical contamination and decommissioning liabilities), or operational incidents could generate costs and reputational damage that offset strategic benefits.

**Dilution Risk:** The Westinghouse acquisition was partially financed with equity. If Cameco pursues further acquisitions or joint ventures (to meet growing nuclear demand), additional dilution is possible.

## 13. Final Assessment

**Rating: HOLD | Price Target: $138.60 (Consensus) | Entry Point for New Buyers: $90-100**

Cameco is an exceptional business in an excellent position within one of the most compelling structural energy themes of the decade. The nuclear renaissance is real. The AI-driven demand for 24/7 carbon-free power is real. The uranium supply deficit is real. Cameco's geological assets are genuinely world-class, and the Westinghouse acquisition transforms the company from a cyclical commodity miner into a comprehensive nuclear energy value-chain company.

None of this is in question. The question is whether $116.75 per share is the right price at which to initiate or add to a position.

At 117.9x trailing P/E and 81.4x EV/EBITDA, Cameco already prices in a substantial portion of the nuclear renaissance. The stock has appreciated 137% in 52 weeks, reflecting the market's recognition of the thesis. The 19% upside to the analyst consensus target of $138.60 is real but modest compensation for the valuation and execution risks that come with owning a mining company at these multiples.

For current shareholders who established positions below $80 (capturing the majority of the 137% run), the calculus is different — holding through the contract repricing cycle makes sense with the stock at these prices, as the long-term EBITDA growth trajectory is intact.

For new investors, the discipline of waiting for a better entry point is the appropriate posture. A pullback to $90-100 per share — which would represent a 15-23% correction from current levels — would offer significantly better risk/reward. Such a pullback is plausible given the valuation, the track record of delivery lumpiness causing earnings misses, and the general rotation risk in high-multiple stocks if bond yields rise or risk sentiment deteriorates.

**The thesis is right. The price is not quite right for new buyers. Watch, wait, and buy the dip.**
