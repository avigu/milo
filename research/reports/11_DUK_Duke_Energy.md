## 1. Overview

Duke Energy is the largest regulated electric utility in the United States by customers served, providing electricity to approximately 8.4 million residential and commercial customers across six states: North Carolina, South Carolina, Indiana, Ohio, Kentucky, and Florida. As the quintessential regulated utility, Duke's business model is built on a straightforward but reliable economic engine — invest capital in generation, transmission, and distribution infrastructure; earn a regulatory-approved rate of return; repeat indefinitely. What sets Duke apart in the current environment is the scale and urgency of the investment opportunity in front of it: AI-driven data center expansion in its primary service territories is reshaping the demand outlook in ways that will drive capital investment — and earnings growth — for a decade or more.

At $124.17, Duke Energy shares trade at what we believe is a compelling valuation: 11.9x EV/EBITDA versus NextEra Energy's 18.8x, a 37% discount that is not justified by fundamentals. The 52-week return of just +3.5% dramatically underperforms the broader utility sector (represented by NextEra's +36.3% return), driven primarily by uncertainty around North Carolina rate cases and the perception that Duke is a slow-moving incumbent in a sector requiring rapid transformation.

That perception is changing. Duke's $75+ billion five-year capital plan, targeting grid hardening, renewable transition, and data center capacity, is the kind of investment that generates reliable earnings growth in the regulated model. Management's consistent delivery — Q1 2026 beat of $0.04, Q3 2025 beat of $0.04, Q4 2025 in-line — demonstrates the operational reliability of the underlying franchise. And the 13% upside to consensus analyst targets of $140, combined with a 3.5% dividend yield, offers total return potential approaching 17% over 12 months — exceptional for a low-beta (0.40) defensive utility.

The central thesis: Duke is a best-valued major utility trading at an unjustified discount to peers, with AI data center tailwinds in its primary service territories, a massive capital investment pipeline that will drive 5–7% annual EPS growth, and a catalyst in sight — resolution of the North Carolina rate case — that could close a significant portion of the valuation gap.

## 2. Strategic Position

Duke Energy's strategic position is built on three interlocking foundations: its regulated geographic franchise, its capital investment capacity, and its accelerating clean energy transition.

**Geographic Franchise:** Duke's service territory encompasses some of the most economically dynamic regions in the U.S. The Carolinas — particularly the Charlotte metro area and the Research Triangle Park corridor (Raleigh-Durham) — have become major technology hubs, attracting financial services companies, pharmaceutical firms, and increasingly, hyperscale data center operators. These areas have experienced above-average population and economic growth for over a decade, and that growth directly translates to electricity load growth within Duke's regulated territory.

The Southeast broadly, and the Carolinas specifically, have become preferred destinations for data center investment. Favorable land costs, business-friendly regulatory environments, and abundant existing transmission infrastructure make the Carolinas attractive to operators who need to rapidly deploy hundreds of megawatts of server capacity. Duke has already filed for significant rate increases citing data center-driven industrial load growth as a primary driver.

Indiana and Ohio provide geographic diversification and exposure to industrial Midwest manufacturing, including EV battery supply chain investments that are driving electricity demand in those states. Florida (Duke Energy Florida) competes in a growing market, though FPL (NextEra's subsidiary) is the dominant player in that state.

**Capital Investment Plan:** Duke's $75+ billion five-year capital plan (2025–2029) is the most important financial fact about the company. In the regulated model, every dollar of approved capital expenditure earns the regulatory-approved rate of return (typically 9–11%). This $75 billion pipeline of investments in grid hardening, renewable generation, EV charging infrastructure, transmission upgrades, and data center interconnection represents an extraordinary earnings engine. A $75 billion capital plan growing the rate base implies annual EPS growth of 5–7% for years — and that projection does not fully incorporate the upside from AI-related demand acceleration.

**Transition Away from Coal:** Duke operates one of the last substantial coal fleets among major U.S. utilities. The planned retirement of Indiana coal plants and their replacement with natural gas, solar, and energy storage is both an environmental imperative and a capital deployment opportunity. Each coal retirement followed by new clean generation triggers regulatory investment that earns the allowed return. The transition creates some near-term regulatory and cost risk but positions Duke well for a zero-carbon future.

## 3. AI Exposure

Duke Energy's AI exposure is high, direct, and arguably underappreciated by the market. The mechanism is simple: AI data centers require enormous amounts of reliable electricity, and Duke is the regulated monopoly provider in regions where data center investment is accelerating at an exceptional pace.

The Charlotte, North Carolina metropolitan area has quietly become one of the five largest data center markets in the United States. Microsoft, Google, Apple, and a range of hyperscale operators have either announced or are actively evaluating major data center developments in the Charlotte region. These facilities are not small — a modern AI training campus requires 200–500 megawatts of power, which can add industrial-scale load to a distribution system in a single project.

Research Triangle Park, Duke's other major North Carolina market, is similarly experiencing rapid data center and technology company expansion. The concentration of universities (Duke, UNC, NC State), pharmaceutical companies, and technology firms in the Raleigh-Durham area has created a talent cluster that naturally attracts technology infrastructure investment.

For Duke, this load growth has a straightforward financial implication. When a large industrial customer requests connection to the grid, Duke must invest in new transmission lines, substation upgrades, and potentially new generation capacity. All of that investment earns the regulated rate of return. In essence, AI data center operators are creating capital deployment opportunities for Duke at Duke's regulated return — a highly attractive proposition.

Duke has also pursued direct partnerships with data center developers to provision dedicated capacity, moving beyond pure reactive infrastructure investment toward proactive business development with large industrial customers. This is a strategic evolution that management deserves credit for — recognizing that data center customers are not just load growth, but business development partners.

Nuclear power is an increasingly important element of Duke's AI story. The company operates nuclear plants in the Carolinas (McGuire and Catawba nuclear stations) that provide 24/7 zero-carbon baseload power. As technology companies increasingly commit to 24/7 carbon-free energy (CFE) — not just annual matching — Duke's nuclear assets become more attractive for corporate power agreements. The ability to offer a regulated utility structure combined with nuclear-based CFE products is a competitive differentiator.

## 4. Competitive Moat

Duke Energy's competitive moat is perhaps the most legally durable in American business: a state-granted monopoly franchise for electric distribution in its service territory. No competitor can legally build competing power lines to serve Duke's customers without state regulatory approval. This franchise creates a captive customer base of 8.4 million accounts that is essentially permanent.

Beyond the franchise monopoly, Duke's moat has several reinforcing dimensions.

**Scale in Capital Execution:** Managing a $75 billion capital plan across six states, multiple regulatory jurisdictions, and diverse asset classes (generation, transmission, distribution, storage) requires organizational capabilities that take decades to build. Duke has thousands of engineers, project managers, regulatory specialists, and financiers who have executed large infrastructure projects at scale. New entrants face not just regulatory barriers but massive organizational learning curves.

**Regulatory Relationships:** Duke has invested decades in building working relationships with six state utility commissions. These relationships — built through consistent rate case filings, community investment, and operational reliability — are a genuine competitive asset. While regulatory relationships do not guarantee favorable outcomes (as the North Carolina rate case uncertainty demonstrates), they do provide Duke with a more constructive starting point than any new entrant could achieve.

**Nuclear Operating Expertise:** Running nuclear power plants safely and economically requires specialized expertise that is scarce in the U.S. Duke's nuclear operations in the Carolinas are among the best-performing in the industry. This expertise is both a moat (creating a high-quality clean energy asset) and a barrier to exit (regulators prefer to keep safe nuclear plants running rather than risk the supply gap created by premature closure).

**Transmission Infrastructure:** Duke's existing transmission network represents over a century of investment and represents a multi-billion dollar sunk cost that competitors cannot efficiently replicate. As data center demand grows, the proximity and capacity of transmission infrastructure becomes a critical siting criterion — and Duke's existing network creates natural connection points for large industrial customers.

## 5. Financial Analysis

Duke Energy's financial profile is that of a mature, well-managed regulated utility — steady, predictable, and undervalued relative to its earnings growth potential.

**Revenue:** TTM revenue of $32.7 billion, growing 6.2% year-over-year, with Q1 2026 growth accelerating to 11.3%. Revenue growth reflects both rate case outcomes (approved rate increases flowing through to customer bills) and volume growth from industrial and residential customer additions. Revenue is the largest of the three utilities we cover, reflecting Duke's scale as the largest regulated utility in the country.

**Margins:** Gross margin of 46.0% is the highest among the three utilities in our coverage (Vistra: 8.7%, NextEra: not directly comparable). This reflects Duke's fully regulated model, where fuel cost pass-through mechanisms protect gross margin even when commodity prices fluctuate. Operating margin of 26.8% and net margin of 15.4% are solid for a regulated utility, though below NextEra's exceptional 29.5% operating margin — reflecting NextEra's more favorable business mix.

**EBITDA:** At $16.5 billion TTM, Duke's EBITDA is the largest of our three covered companies. The EV/EBITDA ratio of 11.9x is substantially below NextEra's 18.8x — a gap that we believe is excessive given the similarity of the regulated utility business model.

**EPS:** TTM EPS of $6.39 represents 9.1% growth year-over-year — solid and consistent. Quarterly execution has been reliable: Q1 2026 beat ($1.93 vs $1.89 estimate), Q3 2025 beat ($1.81 vs $1.77 estimate), Q4 2025 in-line ($1.50 vs $1.50). This pattern of small beats and in-line results is characteristic of a well-managed regulated utility — management guides conservatively and executes reliably.

**Forward P/E:** The 18.8x forward P/E implies consensus EPS expectations of approximately $6.60 for 2026. Duke's five-year capital plan supports earnings growth of 5–7% annually, which would put 2027 EPS at $7.00+ and 2028 EPS at $7.35+. Applied to a 19–20x P/E multiple, this implies a 2028 stock price of $133–$147 — suggesting that the current price is roughly fairly valued on a static basis, and that the investment thesis depends on either (a) multiple expansion (the valuation discount to NEE closing) or (b) capital plan execution driving above-consensus earnings growth.

**Balance Sheet:** Debt/equity of 1.54 is typical for regulated utilities and reflects the capital-intensive nature of the business. Duke's investment-grade credit rating (Baa1/BBB+) provides access to capital markets at competitive rates. ROE of 9.7% is below the utility-sector typical allowed return of 10–11%, suggesting some room for regulatory recovery as pending rate cases are resolved. Net debt is substantial at approximately $65–70 billion, but this is entirely standard for utilities of this scale and is fully supportable by the regulated business model.

**Dividend:** The dividend yield of approximately 3.5% is higher than NextEra (2.5%) and is the most attractive yield among our three covered companies. Duke has grown its dividend for 19 consecutive years and management has committed to continued growth in line with earnings. At $124 per share, the approximately $4.34 annual dividend provides meaningful income support while investors wait for price appreciation.

## 6. Bottlenecks

Duke faces several genuine constraints that investors must monitor closely.

**North Carolina Rate Case Uncertainty:** The North Carolina Utilities Commission (NCUC) has been unpredictable in recent rate proceedings, creating significant investor anxiety. Duke has filed for substantial rate increases to recover its capital investment costs, but the NCUC has at times approved amounts meaningfully below Duke's requests, creating earnings shortfalls relative to management guidance. This regulatory uncertainty is the single largest factor explaining Duke's underperformance relative to peers — and it remains unresolved. Any unfavorable rate case outcome in North Carolina would likely push the stock lower and delay re-rating.

**Indiana Coal Transition Complexity:** Duke's Indiana operations include a significant coal fleet that is being transitioned to gas and renewables. This transition involves complex regulatory proceedings in Indiana (which has historically been more supportive of coal), capital expenditures for new generation, and the risk that coal plant retirements occur before replacement capacity is fully online. Any operational gaps during the transition could impair service reliability and create regulatory liability.

**Execution Risk on $75 Billion Capital Plan:** Executing $75 billion in capital expenditures over five years is operationally demanding. Supply chain disruptions (particularly for transformers, which have multi-year lead times), labor shortages in skilled construction trades, and permitting delays could push project timelines and increase costs. Cost overruns on regulated projects are not automatically recoverable — regulators can disallow costs they deem imprudent.

**Interest Rate Exposure:** While less interest-rate sensitive than NextEra (given Duke's lower multiple), a sustained rise in interest rates would increase Duke's cost of debt refinancing (the company refinances significant amounts annually given its $65+ billion debt load) and could compress multiples by making utility dividends less attractive relative to fixed income.

**Florida Rate Competition:** Duke Energy Florida operates in a state where FPL (NextEra) is the dominant utility, with significant scale advantages. While Duke Florida serves distinct geographic areas under a regulated franchise, it does not benefit from FPL's scale or operational reputation in that state.

## 7. Industry Trends

The regulated utility industry is experiencing a fundamental shift in its growth outlook, driven by trends that are uniformly favorable for large, well-capitalized operators like Duke.

**Data Center Load Growth — The Primary Driver:** As discussed in the AI exposure section, data center demand growth in the Carolinas and Midwest is a structural, multi-decade trend. The Research Triangle Park region alone could add several gigawatts of new load from data center development over the next decade. Each gigawatt of new industrial load requires hundreds of millions in transmission and distribution investment — investment that earns Duke's allowed return.

**Grid Modernization Imperative:** The U.S. electric grid requires massive investment to accommodate renewable generation (which is geographically distributed and intermittent), electric vehicles (which require new distribution capacity), and AI-driven industrial load (which requires large, reliable transmission connections). FERC and state regulators broadly support this investment, creating a regulatory environment where capital spending is encouraged rather than constrained.

**Coal Retirement Wave:** The industry-wide retirement of coal plants is creating capital deployment opportunities for replacement generation and transmission. As older, less efficient coal plants retire, new gas, solar, and storage capacity must be built. In a regulated model, this entire cycle of retirement and replacement generates capital projects that earn the allowed return.

**EV Charging Infrastructure:** Duke has secured regulatory approval to invest in EV charging infrastructure in the Carolinas — a new regulated investment category that earns the allowed return while supporting state climate and transportation goals. As EV penetration grows, charging infrastructure investment will become a meaningful contributor to rate base growth.

**Nuclear Renaissance:** The energy policy environment in 2025–2026 has become increasingly supportive of nuclear power, driven by both the AI power demand story and the recognition that meeting 24/7 clean energy needs requires firm dispatchable generation. Duke's existing nuclear assets benefit from this re-rating of nuclear's role in the clean energy future, and the company is examining its role in next-generation nuclear technology.

## 8. Competitive Landscape

As a regulated utility, Duke's competitive dynamics are fundamentally different from competitive generators like Vistra. Duke does not compete for retail customers within its service territory — it holds a legal monopoly. Competition exists primarily in the regulatory arena (rate case outcomes determined by the state utilities commissions) and in the corporate PPA/industrial customer market.

**Peer Utilities:** Duke's primary U.S. utility peers by scale are NextEra Energy, Southern Company, Dominion Energy, Xcel Energy, and American Electric Power. Among these, NextEra is the clear premium-multiple operator, trading at ~24x P/E versus Duke's 19.5x. The Duke-NextEra valuation gap has historically ranged from 10–30%, and the current 22% gap is toward the wide end of historical norms, suggesting potential for multiple convergence as Duke's rate case uncertainty resolves.

**Southern Company** operates in adjacent Southeast territories (Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi) and is a direct competitive comparison. Southern trades at approximately 20x forward P/E — slightly above Duke — reflecting its constructive regulatory history in Georgia (where rate case outcomes have historically been more favorable than North Carolina) and its stake in Plant Vogtle's new nuclear units.

**Large Industrial Customer Competition:** In the growing market for large data center and industrial customer servicing, Duke competes with Dominion Energy (Virginia, which is the largest U.S. data center market by megawatts) and Southern Company (Georgia, which is also attracting significant technology investment). Duke's Carolinas territory is a genuine competitor to Virginia and Georgia for data center siting decisions — favorable land costs, fiber infrastructure, and Duke's interconnection capacity are real competitive advantages in that discussion.

## 9. Regulatory Risk

Regulatory risk is Duke Energy's most important single risk factor, given that essentially all of its earnings are generated in regulated markets where state utility commissions set the allowed return.

**North Carolina Utilities Commission (NCUC):** The NCUC has been the primary source of investor anxiety. North Carolina has a complex regulatory environment where multiple stakeholder interests — consumer advocates, environmental groups, industrial customers, and low-income advocates — interact in contested rate proceedings. Duke has filed for significant rate increases based on its capital investment plan, and the NCUC's response will determine the pace of earnings growth. An unfavorable outcome — particularly one that disallows a meaningful portion of claimed capital costs or sets the allowed return below Duke's cost of capital — would be a material negative catalyst.

The good news: Duke has an important tool in navigating the North Carolina regulatory environment. The state's Carbon Plan, established by legislation in 2021, explicitly directed Duke to decarbonize its fleet while maintaining reliability — and required regulators to enable Duke's recovery of those investment costs. This legislative mandate provides Duke with some protection against purely political rate case decisions.

**Indiana:** Indiana's regulatory environment has historically been more supportive of the regulated utility model, but the coal transition creates potential for contested proceedings as Duke seeks recovery for retirement costs and replacement generation.

**FERC:** Federal regulation of interstate transmission and wholesale markets creates ongoing exposure to policy changes. FERC's active reform agenda (Order 1920 on long-term transmission planning, ongoing interconnection queue reforms) creates both opportunity (Duke can participate in transmission build-out projects that earn regulated returns) and risk (interconnection policy changes could affect the pace of renewable integration).

**IRA Policy Risk:** Like NextEra, Duke benefits from federal renewable energy tax credits under the IRA for its solar and storage investments. Modification of IRA provisions could reduce the economic attractiveness of renewable capital deployment. However, Duke's regulated model provides more insulation than competitive generators — regulators will allow capital recovery regardless of whether tax credits exist, and rate adjustments can compensate for lost credits over time.

## 10. Valuation

Duke Energy's valuation is the most compelling argument for ownership. At 11.9x EV/EBITDA, Duke is materially undervalued relative to both utility sector history and peer comparisons.

**Peer Multiple Comparison:** NextEra trades at 18.8x EV/EBITDA, implying that a business with fundamentally similar regulated utility economics (FPL) is worth 58% more per dollar of EBITDA than Duke. While some premium for NEER's development optionality is justified, a 58% discount for Duke is excessive. Historical analysis of the NEE-DUK EV/EBITDA spread suggests a fair discount of 20–30%, not 58%.

**P/E Context:** Duke's 19.5x TTM P/E (vs. NextEra's 23.7x) represents a more reasonable premium differential of 22% — consistent with historical patterns. On a forward P/E basis (DUK: 18.8x, NEE: 23.4x), the gap is similar. The EV/EBITDA discount appears anomalous and likely reflects the debt load and rate case uncertainty rather than a fundamentally lower quality business.

**Rate Case Catalyst:** If North Carolina rate cases resolve at or near Duke's requested amounts, we estimate 2026 EPS could exceed consensus by $0.30–$0.50, which on a 19–20x multiple would imply a stock price of $133–$142. This resolution is our primary near-term catalyst.

**DCF Valuation:** At $6.39 TTM EPS growing at 6% annually (midpoint of 5–7% guided range) for 10 years, terminal growth of 2.5%, and discount rate of 8.5%, intrinsic value approximates $140–$155. Our 12-month price target of $142 reflects conservative assumptions in the near term while the rate case uncertainty persists, with upside to the $150+ range if rate cases resolve favorably.

**Total Return Framework:** At $124, an investor receives: 13% price appreciation (to $140 consensus) + 3.5% dividend yield = approximately 16.5% total return over 12 months. For a stock with a 0.40 beta, this represents one of the most attractive Sharpe ratios in the utility sector.

## 11. Risk Analysis

**Primary Risks:**

**North Carolina Rate Case Adverse Outcome (HIGH PROBABILITY OF SOME SHORTFALL, MODERATE-HIGH IMPACT):** The most likely downside scenario for Duke involves the NCUC approving rate increases materially below Duke's requests, creating EPS misses relative to guidance. A scenario where the NCUC disallows 20–25% of claimed capital costs would reduce 2026 EPS by approximately $0.40–$0.60, pushing the stock toward $110–$115. This is a genuine near-term risk, not a tail event.

**Indiana Coal Transition Cost Overruns (MODERATE PROBABILITY, MODERATE IMPACT):** The complexity of retiring coal plants and replacing them with new generation creates execution risk. If new gas plants or solar farms come in over budget, Duke may face a regulatory challenge to recovering full costs.

**Interest Rate Spike (LOW-MODERATE PROBABILITY, MODERATE IMPACT):** A return to a rising rate environment would modestly compress Duke's multiple and increase refinancing costs on a substantial debt load. However, Duke's 0.40 beta and regulated earnings model provide more insulation than market-sensitive sectors.

**Climate Event Risk (LOW PROBABILITY, HIGH IMPACT):** Duke's service territory includes the Southeast and Midwest, both of which experience severe weather. Hurricanes in Florida and ice storms in the Carolinas create operational risk. Duke has invested significantly in grid hardening to mitigate this, but extreme events can cause significant service disruptions and costs.

**Management Transitions (LOW PROBABILITY, LOW-MODERATE IMPACT):** CEO Lynn Good has provided exceptional long-term stability since 2013. Any unexpected leadership transition could create short-term uncertainty, though Duke's operational reliability reflects deep organizational capability that extends beyond any single executive.

## 12. Bull Case / Bear Case

**Bull Case — Target: $155–$165 (25–33% upside)**
The bull case assumes: (1) North Carolina rate cases resolve favorably, with the NCUC approving 90%+ of requested capital cost recovery, enabling EPS guidance of $7.00–$7.20 for 2026; (2) data center load growth in the Carolinas accelerates, requiring Duke to file for emergency rate base additions that are quickly approved; (3) the EV/EBITDA discount to NextEra narrows from 58% to a more historical 25–30%, re-rating Duke's enterprise value; (4) Duke announces a nuclear PPA with a major technology company, adding premium contracted revenue and elevating the nuclear asset valuation narrative. In this scenario, Duke trades at 20–22x forward earnings of $7.00+, implying $140–$154 price range, with further upside from multiple expansion.

**Base Case — Target: $142 (14% upside)**
The base case assumes: (1) North Carolina rate cases resolve at roughly 80% of requested amounts — a partial win; (2) EPS grows 5–6% annually, reaching $6.70–$6.80 in 2026; (3) the EV/EBITDA discount narrows modestly as rate case uncertainty clears; (4) data center load growth continues to develop but does not yet produce a step-change in earnings guidance. Duke re-rates to 20x forward P/E ($142 price), with 3.5% dividend yield providing additional return.

**Bear Case — Target: $105–$112 (10–15% downside)**
The bear case assumes: (1) NCUC disallows a substantial portion of Duke's capital recovery requests, triggering downward EPS revisions of $0.50+; (2) coal transition costs in Indiana come in over budget, creating additional regulatory friction; (3) interest rates rise, compressing the utility multiple from 19.5x to 16–17x; (4) data center buildout in the Carolinas proceeds more slowly than anticipated, reducing the capital deployment opportunity. The stock tests the 52-week low of $111, with the 3.5% dividend providing a partial cushion.

The combination of a 13% price upside to consensus targets and a 3.5% dividend yield creates a total return opportunity of approximately 16–17% over 12 months, delivered by a stock with a 0.40 beta — one of the lowest volatility risk profiles in the equity market. In a world of elevated macroeconomic uncertainty, that ratio of return-to-risk is exceptional.

The central valuation argument is straightforward: a 58% EV/EBITDA discount to NextEra Energy is not justified by fundamentals. Duke is also a regulated utility serving growing service territories with significant capital investment needs. Duke also has nuclear assets, data center exposure, and a clean energy transition underway. The regulatory risk from the NCUC is real but is a temporary overhang on a permanent franchise, not a structural impairment of the business.

The AI data center story in the Carolinas is underappreciated. Charlotte and the Research Triangle are among the most attractive data center development markets in the U.S., and Duke is the monopoly electricity provider. Every megawatt of new data center load that connects to Duke's grid becomes a regulated capital investment opportunity that earns the allowed return. The hyperscale operators moving into North Carolina are not a risk to Duke — they are Duke's most valuable customers.

The catalyst is visible: resolution of the North Carolina rate case proceedings. When the NCUC makes a final determination on Duke's rate applications — expected in the second half of 2026 — it will remove the primary source of uncertainty that has suppressed the stock relative to peers. Even a partial win (80% approval) would likely be received positively by the market given current depressed expectations.

At $124, Duke Energy is the defensive anchor of a utility portfolio — a Dividend Aristocrat-track stock with 3.5% yield, low beta, and 5–7% annual EPS growth embedded in a $75 billion capital plan. Add a realistic 13% price upside as the discount to peers narrows, and this is one of the more compelling risk-adjusted investments in the large-cap equity universe.

The recommendation is BUY with a 12-month target of $142. Investors seeking a stable, growing dividend, modest capital appreciation, and significant downside protection in a market correction will find Duke Energy an ideal position size in any diversified equity or utility portfolio.
