## 1. Overview

Digital Realty Trust, Inc. (NYSE: DLR) is the world's largest wholesale data center REIT, owning and operating a global portfolio of more than 300 data centers spanning approximately 18 countries across six continents. With a market capitalization of approximately $68.6 billion and trailing twelve-month revenue of $6.3 billion, Digital Realty has positioned itself as the primary real estate infrastructure partner for hyperscale cloud providers and large enterprise customers executing massive capacity expansions driven by artificial intelligence workloads.

Digital Realty's business model differs fundamentally from retail colocation peers. Where Equinix serves thousands of enterprises and network operators renting individual cages and cabinets, Digital Realty signs large wholesale leases — typically 1 megawatt and above — to a concentrated roster of hyperscaler and enterprise customers who need entire data halls or entire campuses. This wholesale model creates higher revenue concentration, lumpier GAAP recognition, and greater exposure to individual customer decisions, but it also provides extraordinary leverage to the secular buildout of AI data center infrastructure that is the defining capital expenditure theme of 2025–2030.

The stock trades at $195.31, off a 52-week high of $208.14 but recovering sharply from its 52-week low of $146.23, reflecting a 52-week return of +17.2%. The beta of 1.09 places it slightly above the market — appropriate for a growth-oriented REIT that is more exposed to the AI capex cycle than its peers.

The near-term GAAP earnings record has been noisy. Q3 2025's large miss ($0.15 actual vs. $0.33 expected) created significant investor anxiety, and Q4 2025 also disappointed ($0.24 vs. $0.32). Q1 2026 came in exactly in line ($0.47 vs. $0.47), marking stabilization. The critical context: these GAAP misses do not reflect operational failure. They reflect the timing of when wholesale hyperscaler leases are recognized under GAAP revenue accounting — a structural feature of the wholesale business model, not a deterioration in bookings or customer demand.

The counter-narrative to the GAAP misses is powerful: Digital Realty signed a record $5 billion-plus in annualized contract value (ACV) of new pre-leasing in 2025 — the largest pre-leasing backlog in the company's history. These contracts are signed, deposits are paid, and construction is underway or scheduled. Revenue recognition will follow delivery; the backlog provides the clearest forward visibility of any metric in the data center sector. The market, focused on backward-looking GAAP misses, has underpriced the forward value embedded in this backlog.

We initiate coverage with a **BUY** rating and a 12-month price target of $218, consistent with analyst consensus, representing approximately 12% upside from current levels plus the dividend yield.

## 2. Strategic Position

Digital Realty's strategic position is defined by its role as the real estate infrastructure layer beneath hyperscale AI expansion. To understand the opportunity, it is necessary to understand how the largest technology companies in the world build data centers.

Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Oracle do not build all of their data centers themselves. Each has a division that handles real estate acquisition, construction, and facility management — but each also relies on third-party wholesale data center operators for a significant portion of their capacity, particularly for speed-to-market, geographic diversification, and capital efficiency. The wholesale data center model allows a hyperscaler to lease a 10-megawatt, 100-megawatt, or even multi-hundred-megawatt data hall on a 10–20 year term without deploying its own capital for land, construction, permitting, and power infrastructure — all of which Digital Realty handles.

This model is highly attractive to hyperscalers in an AI buildout environment characterized by time pressure. Building a hyperscaler-owned data center on greenfield land in a new market takes 3–5 years. Leasing an already-under-construction or already-permitted Digital Realty facility can cut that timeline to 12–18 months. In a competitive AI infrastructure race where AWS, Azure, and Google are each committing $60–80 billion annually in capital expenditure, the ability to deploy capacity faster is worth paying a lease premium.

Digital Realty's PlatformDIGITAL strategy articulates this proposition formally. Rather than simply leasing powered shells, PlatformDIGITAL positions DLR as a connected global data center platform — providing not just physical infrastructure but also geographic consistency, compliance capabilities across 18 countries, and the ServiceFabric interconnection layer that allows enterprise customers to maintain consistent network access across DLR's global portfolio. This is a deliberate strategic evolution from commodity wholesale real estate toward connected infrastructure, designed to increase switching costs and justify premium pricing.

The Interxion acquisition (completed in 2020, approximately $8.4 billion) was transformative for DLR's European strategic position. Interxion was the leading carrier-neutral colocation provider in continental Europe — with dense interconnection hubs in Frankfurt, Amsterdam, Paris, and Stockholm — and its integration into the DLR platform created a hybrid wholesale-plus-colocation model in Europe that is unmatched by any single competitor. The combined entity serves enterprise customers that want wholesale capacity alongside interconnection connectivity, all under a single provider.

Digital Realty's customer concentration is notable. Amazon is estimated to represent approximately 10% of total revenue; Microsoft, Meta, and Oracle collectively add significant further concentration. This concentration is a double-edged sword: it provides revenue visibility and pricing leverage with tier-1 counterparties, but it also means that a single major customer's capex delay or strategic pivot can meaningfully affect quarterly results — as the GAAP earnings record demonstrates.

## 3. AI Exposure

Digital Realty's AI exposure is among the highest of any publicly traded REIT, and arguably among the highest of any infrastructure company in any sector.

The mechanism is direct: every major hyperscaler AI data center buildout — every GPU cluster being deployed by Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Meta AI, and Oracle Cloud — requires data center infrastructure. Some of this infrastructure is hyperscaler-owned. A growing and significant portion is leased from third-party wholesale data center operators, and Digital Realty is the largest and most global of those operators.

The scale of AI-driven hyperscaler capex is difficult to overstate. In 2025, the five largest hyperscalers committed a combined estimated $400 billion in capital expenditure, the majority of which was directed at data center infrastructure. Even if DLR captures a small fraction of this buildout through wholesale leases, the revenue impact is substantial. The record $5 billion-plus ACV pre-leasing signed in 2025 demonstrates that this is not a theoretical exposure — DLR is already capturing the contracts.

The power density demands of AI workloads represent both an opportunity and an engineering challenge. AI GPU clusters require power densities of 50–150 kW per rack, well above the 8–12 kW per rack that characterized data center design a decade ago. Digital Realty is actively designing and constructing facilities capable of these densities — including liquid cooling infrastructure, upgraded power distribution systems, and purpose-built mechanical designs. The capital investment required to meet AI-grade power density is substantial, but it also creates a competitive barrier: not all existing data center operators have the engineering capability or balance sheet to retrofit existing facilities or build new ones at AI-grade specs.

Build-to-suit agreements — where a hyperscaler pre-commits to lease a DLR facility that does not yet exist — are the most powerful manifestation of AI-driven demand. In a build-to-suit arrangement, DLR secures a customer commitment (often with a deposit) before breaking ground, eliminating speculative construction risk and providing revenue certainty from the first day of occupancy. The record 2025 pre-leasing backlog is predominantly built on this model.

The European AI buildout is an underappreciated component of DLR's AI exposure. European cloud regions are expanding rapidly as the EU's AI regulatory framework (the EU AI Act) creates compliance requirements for cloud providers serving European customers. Data sovereignty rules mandate that certain AI workloads be processed within EU borders. DLR's European footprint — enhanced significantly by the Interxion acquisition — positions it uniquely to serve this demand.

## 4. Competitive Moat

Digital Realty's moat is rooted in scale, global platform continuity, power access, and customer relationships, rather than the network-effects moat that characterizes Equinix's interconnection business.

**Global Scale and Platform Consistency.** Operating 300+ data centers across 18 countries under a unified operating model, compliance framework, and network platform (ServiceFabric) is operationally complex and extremely capital-intensive to replicate. A hyperscaler signing a build-to-suit agreement with DLR in Singapore, Frankfurt, and Dallas simultaneously — which is a common transaction — receives consistent legal documentation, operating standards, power specifications, and network connectivity across three continents. No other wholesale operator can match this geographic breadth under a consistent platform.

**Pre-Leasing Relationships.** The relationships between Digital Realty and its hyperscaler customers — Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, Oracle — have been built over years of successful project delivery. Hyperscalers developing a new AI campus in a new geography default to existing suppliers with proven track records. The switching cost is not technological (as in Equinix's interconnection business) but reputational and relational: a hyperscaler that shifts to an untested data center partner risks delivery delays, compliance failures, and operational inconsistencies across a multi-hundred-million-dollar deployment.

**Power Infrastructure.** Securing power in major data center markets is the most challenging supply constraint in the industry. Digital Realty has executed a multi-year strategy of signing Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) with utilities and renewable energy developers, securing power commitments years ahead of need. This pre-positioned power access — in markets like Northern Virginia (the world's largest data center concentration), Chicago, Dallas, London, Frankfurt, and Singapore — is a competitive asset that requires years and significant organizational capability to replicate.

**Interxion Interconnection Hub Access.** The Interxion assets in continental Europe provide DLR's wholesale customers with access to dense interconnection ecosystems — particularly in Amsterdam's AMS-IX and Frankfurt's DE-CIX, two of the world's highest-traffic internet exchange points. This hybrid positioning (wholesale capacity adjacent to interconnection density) is a competitive differentiator in Europe that pure-wholesale US competitors cannot match.

## 5. Financial Analysis

Digital Realty's financial profile reflects the structural characteristics of a high-growth wholesale REIT in an active capital deployment phase. The headline numbers must be interpreted through the correct accounting lens to be meaningful.

**Revenue.** Revenue of $6.3 billion for the trailing twelve months represents 12.6% year-over-year growth, with Q1 2026 accelerating to +16.7% — the strongest quarterly growth rate in several years. This acceleration is driven by the delivery and commencement of leases signed in the 2024–2025 pre-leasing cycle, and it validates the forward revenue embedded in the record backlog.

**GAAP Earnings — The Right Interpretation.** Digital Realty's GAAP earnings are subject to significant distortion from depreciation on data center assets, straight-line lease adjustments, and the timing of wholesale lease commencement. A 10-megawatt lease signed in Q2 may not be recognized as revenue until Q4 when the facility is delivered and the customer takes occupancy. This structural lumpiness in GAAP revenue recognition is the primary driver of the Q3 2025 miss (EPS $0.15 vs. $0.33 expected) and Q4 2025 miss (EPS $0.24 vs. $0.32 expected). These misses do not reflect customers canceling leases, pricing compression, or operational failures.

**Core FFO.** The appropriate valuation metric for Digital Realty is Core Funds From Operations (Core FFO), which adjusts for non-cash depreciation, amortization, and other items to reflect the cash generated by the portfolio. Core FFO per share is estimated at approximately $6–7, placing the stock at a P/FFO of approximately 28–33x at the current price of $195.31. This is consistent with Equinix's AFFO multiple and with the premium warranted by DLR's growth profile.

**Margins.** Gross margin of 57.5% is strong and reflective of DLR's ability to achieve premium pricing with hyperscaler customers on long-term leases. Operating margin of 11.4% is compressed by depreciation on the rapidly expanding asset base, but EBITDA margin (EBITDA of $2.89B on $6.3B revenue) of approximately 45.9% is healthy and expanding as newly delivered capacity fills.

**Return on Equity.** ROE of 6.0% is modest relative to other industries but appropriate for a capital-intensive REIT. The balance sheet carries a debt-to-equity ratio of 0.80, lower than Equinix's 1.24 on a GAAP basis, though DLR's absolute debt load is substantial in dollar terms given the capital intensity of its expansion program.

**EPS Growth.** The 211.7% year-over-year GAAP EPS growth is a base-effect artifact and is not a meaningful indicator of underlying business acceleration. Core FFO per share growth of 8–12% annually is the relevant trajectory.

**Capital Expenditure.** Digital Realty is in an active development cycle, deploying billions annually in new data center construction. This capex is largely covered by pre-leasing commitments, PPAs, and long-term customer contracts, reducing speculative risk. But it also means that free cash flow is significantly below EBITDA in the near term as the company invests in capacity.

## 6. Bottlenecks

**Power Procurement.** Power availability remains the most critical constraint on Digital Realty's ability to deliver against its backlog. In Northern Virginia — the world's most active data center market, where DLR has its largest US concentration — Dominion Energy and other utilities have faced grid congestion issues that have slowed new power connections. In Europe, similar dynamics apply. DLR's PPA program and direct utility relationships are actively addressing this, but power procurement timelines of 2–4 years create a persistent lag between pre-leasing commitments and revenue recognition.

**Construction Supply Chain.** AI-grade data center construction requires specialized components: high-efficiency transformers, advanced cooling systems (including liquid cooling for GPU-dense deployments), and electrical distribution equipment. Global supply chains for these components were strained through 2024–2025, and lead times remain extended. Any further supply chain disruption — geopolitical, logistical, or weather-related — would delay construction timelines and push GAAP revenue recognition further into the future.

**Customer Concentration Risk.** Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, and Oracle collectively represent a very significant share of Digital Realty's pre-leasing backlog. If any one of these customers materially reduces capex — due to AI investment disappointment, regulatory pressure, or economic slowdown — the impact on DLR's forward revenue would be material. This is not a probability we assign high likelihood, given the structural nature of AI infrastructure competition, but it is a risk that is structurally unavoidable given DLR's wholesale business model.

**Debt Service and Capital Markets Access.** Digital Realty's expansion program requires ongoing access to debt and equity capital markets. In an environment of elevated interest rates, the cost of new debt issuance increases, compressing the spread between DLR's return on invested capital and its cost of capital. The company must continuously access markets to fund its pipeline, creating sensitivity to capital market conditions that pure-equity companies do not face.

## 7. Industry Trends

**Hyperscale AI Buildout.** The capital expenditure cycle for AI data center infrastructure is the dominant demand driver for Digital Realty and will remain so through at least 2030. Consensus forecasts project that hyperscaler AI capex will grow at 20–30% annually through the end of the decade, driven by competition among the major cloud providers, the proliferation of AI applications, and the compounding power demands of increasingly capable AI models.

**Power Density Evolution.** The average power density of new data center leases has doubled in the past three years and is projected to double again by 2030 as GPU clusters become more powerful. This trend is dramatically increasing the capital intensity of each megawatt of capacity delivered — which is inflationary for data center operators that can pass through higher construction costs in lease rates, and deflationary for operators that cannot. Digital Realty, with its hyperscaler customer relationships and PlatformDIGITAL positioning, is well-placed to price new-generation high-density leases at rates reflecting the increased infrastructure investment.

**Geographic Diversification of AI Infrastructure.** Governments in Europe, Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and India are actively incentivizing the development of local AI data center capacity, both through direct subsidies and through regulatory mandates for data sovereignty. This trend creates demand for exactly the type of global platform Digital Realty operates — a consistent, compliant, multinational data center partner with local relationships, permitting expertise, and power infrastructure in place.

**Wholesale vs. Retail Sector Dynamics.** The distinction between wholesale and retail colocation has become more important as AI workloads increasingly require dedicated large-block capacity. While Equinix's interconnection moat remains uniquely powerful, the largest AI compute deployments are happening in wholesale facilities — which means the capital flowing into AI infrastructure is disproportionately flowing into DLR's addressable market relative to retail colocation.

## 8. Competitive Landscape

Digital Realty's wholesale competitive landscape is significantly less fragmented than retail colocation.

**Primary US Competitors.** In the US wholesale market, CyrusOne (private), Switch (private), and QTS Realty (private, owned by Blackstone) compete with DLR in specific markets. None has DLR's global scale, PlatformDIGITAL consistency, or depth of hyperscaler relationships. In Northern Virginia — the most contested US market — DLR competes primarily with hyperscaler-owned capacity and with Equinix's wholesale/xScale offering.

**Equinix xScale.** Equinix's xScale joint venture is the most direct overlap with DLR's business. xScale targets the same hyperscaler customer base with purpose-built wholesale facilities co-developed with sovereign wealth fund capital. However, xScale is a relatively new and smaller program compared to DLR's established wholesale business; DLR's revenue base and pre-leasing backlog are substantially larger. The two companies are more complementary (DLR on wholesale compute; EQIX on interconnection) than directly substitutable.

**Hyperscaler Self-Build.** The most important potential competitor is the hyperscalers themselves. Amazon, Microsoft, and Google each own substantial proprietary data center capacity and are expanding it rapidly. The decision to lease from DLR versus build directly depends on factors including speed-to-market, capital efficiency, geographic flexibility, and risk appetite. Evidence from the 2025 pre-leasing cycle suggests that even as hyperscaler self-build accelerates, third-party wholesale leasing is growing simultaneously — the market is large enough for both.

**International.** In Europe, competitors include Vantage Data Centers, atNorth (Nordic markets), and various national telecommunications operators. In Asia-Pacific, GDS Holdings and ST Telemedia compete in specific markets. DLR's competitive advantage in each international market rests on its PlatformDIGITAL consistency and the relationships established through the Interxion acquisition.

## 9. Regulatory Risk

**US Tax Policy.** As a REIT, Digital Realty must distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders. Any change in REIT tax treatment would affect DLR's cost of capital and capital allocation flexibility. Legislative proposals in the US have periodically targeted data center REITs specifically, arguing that digital infrastructure is not "real estate" in the traditional sense. While no such legislation has passed, the risk is non-zero and is worth monitoring.

**EU AI Act and Data Sovereignty.** The EU AI Act (fully effective 2025) and ongoing data sovereignty mandates create compliance obligations for hyperscaler customers that translate into demand for EU-located data center capacity — a net positive for DLR's European business. However, the regulatory environment also creates operational complexity and potential for disruption if compliance requirements shift materially.

**Environmental Regulation.** Data center energy consumption is under increasing regulatory scrutiny globally. In Ireland, the Netherlands, and Singapore — three key DLR markets — regulators have imposed or considered restrictions on new data center connections due to grid and carbon concerns. DLR's PPA program partially mitigates this risk by demonstrating renewable energy procurement, but restrictions on absolute energy consumption would limit capacity additions in constrained markets.

**Antitrust and Market Power.** As data center concentration increases globally, antitrust regulators may scrutinize acquisitions or market positions that could raise barriers to entry. DLR's Interxion acquisition was reviewed by EU competition authorities and approved with conditions. Future acquisitions may face similar or heightened scrutiny.

## 10. Valuation

Digital Realty's GAAP-based P/E metrics are not the appropriate valuation framework. The forward P/E of 95.0x reflects GAAP depreciation distorting the earnings denominator; the trailing P/E of 49.8x similarly reflects lumpy revenue recognition.

**Core FFO.** At estimated Core FFO of $6–7 per share and a price of $195.31, P/FFO is approximately 28–33x. This is consistent with Equinix's AFFO multiple and with comparable wholesale data center REITs. The premium to traditional REIT multiples (15–20x) is justified by DLR's growth rate (12.6% revenue growth, projected 10–15% FFO growth as pre-leasing backlog converts) and the secular demand tailwind from AI infrastructure.

**EV/EBITDA.** At 22.2x EV/EBITDA, DLR trades at a modest discount to Equinix's 32.1x, reflecting Equinix's superior margin profile and interconnection network effects. The discount is appropriate but may compress as DLR's AI pre-leasing backlog converts to recognized EBITDA over 2026–2028.

**NAV Analysis.** Data center real estate has been valued in private markets at approximately $8–12 million per megawatt of critical IT load, depending on market, power density, and lease structure. Digital Realty's approximately 2,600 MW of capacity implies a replacement-cost NAV meaningfully above current equity market value, providing a margin of safety that underpins the BUY rating even in a more conservative scenario.

**Analyst Consensus.** The consensus price target of $217.86 reflects agreement across sell-side analysts on the FFO-based framework and the forward value of the pre-leasing backlog. Our $218 target is consistent with consensus and represents approximately 12% upside.

**Dividend.** DLR pays approximately $5.00 per share annually, implying a dividend yield of approximately 2.6% at current prices. Including the dividend, the total return to our 12-month price target is approximately 14–15%.

## 11. Risk Analysis

**GAAP Miss Risk (Medium).** The pattern of GAAP earnings misses — Q3 2025 (-$0.18 vs. consensus), Q4 2025 (-$0.08 vs. consensus) — creates investor credibility risk even when the misses are timing-related rather than operational. If Q2 or Q3 2026 produces additional GAAP misses, even for legitimate accounting reasons, the stock could re-test lower levels as institutional holders reduce position size.

**Customer Concentration (High).** Amazon at approximately 10% of revenue means that a single large customer represents a material portion of the revenue base. If Amazon's AI capex cycle slows — whether due to technology risk, competitive disruption, or financial pressure — DLR's booking activity and revenue growth would be materially affected.

**Interest Rate Sensitivity (Medium).** As a REIT with a large and growing debt load, Digital Realty is sensitive to interest rate movements. Rate increases compress the spread between ROIC and cost of capital, and may compress the FFO multiple the market awards.

**Construction and Delivery Risk (Medium-High).** The record pre-leasing backlog is only valuable if DLR delivers on schedule and on budget. Power procurement delays, construction supply chain disruptions, or permitting challenges in key markets could delay delivery, push revenue recognition further out, and create additional GAAP misses.

**Power and Environmental Regulation (Medium).** Restrictions on data center power consumption in key markets — particularly in Europe — could limit DLR's ability to expand in the highest-demand markets. This risk is partially mitigated by DLR's PPAs and utility relationships, but it is not fully eliminable.

**Leverage.** While Debt/Equity of 0.80 is moderate relative to Equinix, the absolute dollar value of DLR's debt is substantial. In a scenario of rapid revenue decline (e.g., a significant hyperscaler customer loss), the fixed debt service obligations could strain the balance sheet.

GAAP earnings stabilize and the miss pattern resolves as delivered capacity catches up with bookings. Revenue growth sustains in the 12–16% range. ## 13. Final Assessment

Digital Realty's investment case rests on a single most important data point: the record $5 billion-plus ACV pre-leasing backlog signed in 2025, representing the largest forward contracted revenue visibility in the company's history. This backlog exists because the largest technology companies in the world — Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, Oracle — are committing years in advance to wholesale data center capacity that they need for AI infrastructure. They are paying to secure DLR's capacity precisely because it is scarce, because DLR's global platform is uniquely capable, and because the AI buildout timeline cannot wait for greenfield self-build.

The GAAP earnings misses of 2025 — Q3 2025's -$0.18 and Q4 2025's -$0.08 versus consensus — were real disappointments in the near term and created justifiable investor anxiety. But they were not indicators of business deterioration. They were artifacts of GAAP revenue recognition timing in a wholesale leasing business that is booking faster than it can deliver. The Q1 2026 in-line result marks the beginning of normalization as delivery catches up with the 2024–2025 booking surge.

At 22.2x EV/EBITDA and approximately 28–33x Core FFO, Digital Realty is not cheap, but it is not unreasonably valued for a business growing revenue at 12–17% with record forward bookings, a global platform moat, and the highest AI infrastructure leverage of any publicly traded REIT. The 12% upside to analyst consensus plus the 2.6% dividend yield provides a total return of approximately 14–15% over the next 12 months.

**Rating: BUY. Price Target: $218. Digital Realty is the wholesale infrastructure landlord of the AI buildout. The market is discounting timing noise; the backlog is the signal.**
